Table 1 lists the 14 different coins used in this project. Identical pricing data was gathered for each, although the period over which data was available differed. This is due to the novelty of cryptocurrencies, with Bitcoin being first released in 2009 while several others were only created in more recent years. Generally, data was recorded as far back in time as to the beginning of each coin.

Data was recorded in long format, with each entry representing a unique observation of a given cross section at a given time. In this case, cross sections represent different coins while the timing of each observation was for a given minute. Since certain coins have already existed for several years, there was a large amount of data with tens of millions of observations. 

Because of the combination of different cross sections at different times, it is possible to interpret this as a panel dataset. However, for a useful result, it is more important to understand the relationship through time of the given variables rather than one which may be time invariant. Therefore, any panel dataset analysis or methods were not pursued.

Table 2 gives a description of the different variables available for use in forecasting. The outcome variable, Target, was based on a 15 minute return. Put another way, Target describes the return on investment one would receive if a certain coin was purchased in the current minute and then sold 15 minutes into the future. Although the description implies comparing prices at two specific points in time, the providers of the dataset actually used a moving average with a 3750 minute window when creating Target. This was done presumably to smooth the variable, so that outliers or noise from sudden price changes wouldn’t too greatly harm the forecasting task.
 
Two of the variables, Count and Volume, relate to the amount of each coin that was traded during the minute. Four of the variables, Open/Close and High/Low, relate to actual pricing information. VWAP combines the two in providing pricing information that is weighted by the amount traded. Finally, since the data is in long format, both a time and cross sectional index are present in the two remaining variables.
 
In order to more easily evaluate performance and compare methods, the 14 coin forecasts and ground truth values were aggregated into a single metric. Specifically, a weighted version of the Pearson correlation coefficient was used with each coin having a unique weight.

Table 3 shows the weights of each coin rounded to the nearest tenth decimal place. A larger weight causes the correlation between that coin’s forecasted and true values to more greatly affect the final correlation metric. As the weights were provided with the data, they may be adjusted depending on modeling goals. In this project, Bitcoin and Ethereum had the greatest weights. 
 
Notable high level information found in the data included the following:
  • There was a total of over 24 million rows, with each row representing a unique observation of a given coin at a given time as mentioned above
  • There was an uneven number of observations for each coin. Generally the first observations for each coin coincided with when the coin was first created, although some were released several years after others. However, all observations had the same ending timestamp.
  • Although data was recorded at the minute level, gaps sometimes existed between observations of the same coin. About 400,000 observations had gaps between timestamps across all coins. The largest gap was five minutes, while the most common gap was two minutes.
  • For all recorded rows, almost all missing values in individual variables were in the outcome variable Target. This was likely due to it being a 15 minute return, that by nature depended on price information at multiple points in time. There were about 750,000 missing values for Target across all coins, most of which can be explained by the 400,000 gaps between consecutive timestamps as mentioned above.

With time series data, additional assumptions and techniques are needed to build an appropriate model that can forecast future returns. In particular, data from the past is only useful if it is expected that similar relationships should hold true into the future. Often, using all available historical data is not appropriate as it can create models are based on irrelevant information or that are not accurate in the short term. Because of this, only the two most recent years worth of data was used in order to produce a forecast on the final three months of data.

In addition to proper selection of data, particular assumptions of time series models must be met. The key among these is stationarity, where the statistics of the data should be constant across any selected time period.

Figure 1, above, shows the outcome variable Target over the two year period with the mean value for each coin in red. As mentioned, the two year period includes both the training and testing sets with the final three months in the data being the testing set. It can be seen that all Target series appear to have a constant mean, meeting part of the requirement for stationarity. The variance of each series also generally appears to be constant, although there are some slight outliers. For example, several series have certain segments with greater variance compared to others. Maker and Dogecoin, for example, seem to have higher variance in the second half of the series than the first. At other short intervals, Target also appears to be extremely large and positive. Examples of this can be seen in April 2020 for IOTA and Monero. These short term, large positive returns are seen in most coins’ series, but their duration may make them able to be ignored. For the most part, however, the series also appear stationary in the variance. 

While a visual or manual inspection of stationarity is important, it is also useful to perform a formal hypothesis test. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller test was used to do so. This evaluates the null hypothesis that a time series is characterized by a unit root process, implying non-stationarity. The alternative hypothesis is then that the process is characterized by a non-unit root, implying stationarity. The test functions by fitting an autoregressive model to the data and evaluating the significance of the first lag coefficient. If the series has a unit root, the coefficient should be one as previous information does not influence future values anymore than being additive. If the series has a non-unit root, the coefficient should be less than one as previous values cause reversion to the mean making the process stationary.

When performing these tests, the large amount of data motivated finding faster ways to compute the test. To this end, three different methods of performing the hypothesis test were evaluated. The first used the entire two year period without any changes to obtain the most reliable answer to the hypothesis test. The second subset the data by taking every 20th value and evaluating the hypothesis test on the result. The final method took a random, ordered sample of 5% of the total data and likewise evaluated the hypothesis test on the result. For all coins, all three methods rejected the null hypothesis that the outcome variable of Target was characterized by a unit root. The alternative hypothesis of the series containing a non-unit root was failed to be rejected. Therefore, as found visually, the outcome variable was stationary. 

An identical procedure was performed for all other continuous variables for all coins. These variables included Open/Close, High/Low, Volume, and VWAP. When a given variable was tested, all coins shared the same result for that variable in either rejecting or failing to reject the null hypothesis. Unlike Target, Volume was the only variable out of this group of six which was already stationary. Open/Close, High/Low, and VWAP were all non-stationary.

Figure 2 shows the Open variable for the two years of the dataset used in modeling. The red line indicates the mean value of the variable for each coin. Unlike Target in Figure 1, Open is clearly non-stationary. The opening price of each coin clearly increases at later times in the dataset and experiences greater variance as prices rise and fall with greater magnitude. This agrees with the result of the hypothesis test for this variable, along with Close, High/Low, and VWAP which were also found to be non-stationary. Intuitively, these variables should be non-stationary as cryptocurrencies have increased greatly in price and are also known for their wilder swings. This also means, however, that the variables should be made stationary if used in time series models.

Figure 3 shows the same Open variable as Figure 2 but after transformations were applied to make it stationary. As is commonly done, a logarithm was applied to improve the stationarity in the variance. After the logarithm, a first difference was applied to then improve the stationarity in the mean. Note that when these two transformations are used together, the order of logarithm first and differencing second is meaningful. This is to avoid undefined values if a logarithm of a negative number were to be taken, which can be produced after a differencing operation.

After these transformations, almost all values are centered around the mean. The magnitude of the variable, shown on the y-axis, is generally smaller than in Figure 2. Certain isolated points, not clearly visible, are also now extremely different in magnitude than the bulk of the data. This causes the scale on the graphs to appear zoomed out.  

The same stationarity hypothesis tests were then applied to the transformed Open variable along with the transformed five others that were found to be originally non-stationary. Based on the results of the untransformed tests, a subset of the data taking every 20th value was used to improve computation speed while delivering similar results. The transformed variables were found to be stationary for all coins, implying that the logarithm and differencing operations were adequate. An identical procedure was repeated separately for the Count variable, which is a numeric variable but discrete rather than continuous. Without an already existing implementation, the discrete nature of the variable was ignored for stationarity testing. When doing so, it was also found that the same transformations described previously also turned Count from a non-stationary to stationary series.
